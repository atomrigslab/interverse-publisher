National treasure
Celadon water dropper in the shape of mother monkey with baby
This small celadon water dropper takes the shape of a squatting monkey cradling a young one, as its tiny hands caresses the mother’s face and chest as if to feel the warmth of her love. The jasper-blue and crystal-clear hue, smooth surface texture, and gentle curves come together to ensure an unparalleled sense of presence. This water dropper is one of the twenty celadons that Kansong Chun Hyungpil acquired in 1937 from John Gadsby, a British lawyer and also globally renowned celadon collector in Tokyo, Japan. As a cultural asset, this piece effectively exemplifies the aesthetics of mimetic Goryeo celadon.
National treasure
Blue-and-white porcelain bottle with chrysanthemum design in underglaze iron and copper
This Joseon dynasty’s white porcelain features understated decorations in a variety of colors against a pristine white backdrop. The elegant curve of its slender neck sits on a voluptuous body, which features a celadon-infused orchid along with insets decked in iron paint and copper-red pigments (Dongwha, Jinsa). An epitome of 18th-century Joseon white porcelain in its superb instantiation of rustic style and taste, this piece is also notable for the story behind its acquisition. At an auction held at the Gyeongseong Art Club in 1936, Kansong Chun Hyungpil outbid the Japanese company Yamanaka & Co. with 14,580 won, marking record price for white porcelain at that time.
National treasure
Pair of stone tigers
A pair of stone tiger statues, of smooth silhouettes. The round face, wide-eyed glare, and the equivocal expression bordering on both anger and amusement create a candid yet humorous feel. Entrusted with the power to ward off evil spirits in the folkloric tradition, these tigers stand guard in the gardens of the Kansong Art Museum. Records show that Kansong Chun Hyungpil purchased them at an auction held at Gyeongseong Art Club on March 3, 1935.
